Learn a new language

You probably tried to learn a foreign language before, whether you did or not, I’ll try to make this time easier and faster.

What problem does this address?

Many learners start a new language without structure; they do it randomly—apps here, videos there, zero structure. They “study” for months and still can’t understand or speak. leading to slow progress, frustration, and eventual burnout. And I’ll try my best not make this happen to you.

Why do it? What will you gain?

Because language unlocks people, cultures, and opportunities. It changes how you think, how you learn, and how you connect with the world.
It’s also one of the few skills where progress compounds fast if you’re consistent—even a little every day goes a long way.

Now let’s talk about real communication skills first: understanding, speaking, reading, and writing. Confidence comes next—you stop being scared of mistakes. You also gain sharper memory, better pattern recognition, and a deeper understanding of how learning itself works.

How to do it?

Start small and structured. Learn core vocabulary and basic grammar, then immediately apply them through listening and reading. Add speaking early, even if it’s just talking to yourself. Use simple content at first, then gradually increase difficulty. Don’t wait to “feel ready”—usage is what makes things stick.

How to stay consistent?

Set a daily goal you know you can do: maybe mix in different skills in one day, like reading and writing, and listening in one day. You do your own schedule! Make it non-negotiable. Track what you do so progress feels visible. Accept mistakes as part of the process—every error is feedback, not failure.

Why are 30 days enough to start it?

30 days is enough to build a solid foundation and a habit. You’ll recognize common words, understand basic structures, and feel the language becoming familiar. That early momentum is what turns “trying a language” into actually learning one. You’re not magically becoming “fluent” in one month, but if you put in the work and dedication, you will be at a decent level.

What does success look like after 30 days?

Let’s be real. I can’t tell you what success would look like after 30 days. It depends on many things, including the language itself and how devoted you are to it. To try to guess, I think that if you study 6 hours a day SMARTLY, you would understand songs and about 40% of a video, and you would be able to form sentences, not all of them basic. You would be able to have short conversations. All of that in one month! I hope…

What usually goes wrong

Inconsistency, passive learning only (just watching or scrolling), and fear of speaking. Another big issue is trying to learn everything at once. Focus on basics first, then build upward.

What system or free resources help?

Now comes the big part.

  1. FOR READING
    LingQ
    tons of free stories with audio and vocab tools (many languages).
    Readlang
    read web pages or texts with instant click-to-translate; builds flashcards.
    Duolingo Stories
    free short, interactive stories.
    The Spanish experiment (Spanish specific)
    fun stories, and they actually have course reviews and beginner lessons!
    Fluent with stories (Spanish specific)
    free Spanish stories with different levels.
    And of course, if you get into an intermediate level, you can read novels and books that don’t aim for learners.

  2. FOR WRITING
    My advice here would be to write every single day. Write about literally anything, your day, your dreams, ANYTHING. But you must do it everyday and correct it with and correct it with an AI tool. If you think that there’s nothing to write about, this never happens; there’s always something to write about, maybe ask an AI tool what to write today.
    LanguageTool
    f it’s an AI tool to check your grammatical errors and spelling mistakes across languages. Free of course.

  3. FOR LISTENING
    Easy Languages
    This is a YouTube channel, and they go to the streets and ask people different questions in different languages, and of course, there’s translation. They have a specific channel for every language, for example: Easy German, Easy Spanish, Easy French, Easy Mandarin, Easy Korean and Japanese (playlists), and other languages! (And they have a website for every language).
    Raquel de la Morena (Spanish specific)
    It’s not aimed at learners, but it’s a YouTube channel that makes very interesting videos about lots of topics
    Anna Cramling Español (Spanish specific)
    A YouTuber making educational chess content in Spanish, if you would like to learn two things at once!
    Linguriosa (Spanish specific)
    It’s not aimed at learners, but it’s a YouTube channel that makes very interesting videos about lots of topics.
    Lingoni GERMAN (German specific)
    A YouTube channel aimed at learners with different levels. Very entertaining topics(and I think there’s another version for French and other languages).

  4. FOR SPEAKING
    HelloTalk
    Chat + voice messages with native speakers for free.
    Tandem
    Language exchange with text, audio, and calls
    Discord Language Servers
    You can just search, for example: “Spanish learning discord” or whatever language you want to learn, and you’ll find live voice rooms for real-time speaking practice.
    Or you can just speak to an AI if you’re shy, or speak in a video and see your level and your mistakes. However, I highly recommend that you speak to real people; it’s not that scary or embarrassing. Once you speak one or two times, it just becomes normal and not embarrassing. And don’t worry if you make mistakes, people use these apps and sites because they make mistakes, and they want to learn.

  5. FOR MEMORIZING
    ClozeMaster
    It’s super beneficial. It has endless questions about literally everything: vocabulary, grammar, tricky words, slang, the most popular 100 words, then 500, then 1000, and so on. You must use it if you want to learn any language.
    Anki (flash cards)
    it’s a library of decks of words in different languages! You can make your own decks or just take online decks. A deck contains different words, and you should memorize them and repeat them till you memorize them with no problems.
    Make sentences with the words you are learning, make them practical sentences, so you don't forget them. Personally, I think that’s enough; those were the only things I used to memorize any word.

  6. FOR GRAMMAR
    Lingolia
    It's a great resource for learning grammar rules and practicing exercises in a structured way.

    ClozeMaster
    It’s super beneficial. It has endless questions about literally everything: vocabulary, grammar, tricky words, slang, the most popular 100 words, then 500, then 1000, and so on. You must use it if you want to learn any language.
  7. SPECIFIC COURSES
    Learn Spanish with SpanishPod101
    This is a YouTube channel that has an endless amount of useful educational videos about everything connected to Spanish. And they also have lots of courses.

    8. DW Learn German
    Literally the best website to learn German. It has many courses for every level.
    Nicos Weg
    This is my favorite course on “DW learn German”. It’s like a tv show, a Spanish guy lost in German and you’re both trying to learn German. You’ll learn with every episode, and after every episode, there will be tests, new words, and grammar. And there are also like seasons to it, like the first season is for A1 level, the second season is for A2 level, etc. Lowkey the show itself is interesting even if you’re not going to learn.

    And of course, the free courses are literally endless on YouTube and on the internet in general. Just search for what you want to learn!

What transferable lesson does this teach?

How to learn anything long-term. Discipline, patience, pattern recognition, and trust in gradual progress. You learn that fluency isn’t magic—it’s built step by step, the same way any serious skill is. (And also you learn a new language).
