Teaching/Mentoring skills

Teaching others is one of the quickest ways to grow, both as a person and in the skill itself. You don’t need to be an expert or have years of experience—just be a few steps ahead and open to sharing.

And in many cases, the teacher ends up gaining even more than the student. It’s a genuinely rewarding and enjoyable process.

What problem does this address?

Many people want a teacher but struggle to find one who’s available, affordable, or genuinely good.
Others rely on random videos with no feedback. Mentoring fills that gap by offering direct guidance, explanations, and accountability that most learners don’t have access to.

Why do it? What will you gain?

When you teach a skill, you get better at it. Explaining concepts forces you to organize your thoughts, notice gaps in your understanding, and refine how you apply the skill. You stop relying on intuition and start understanding why things work.
In many cases, the teacher improves faster than the student.
Mentoring also fosters empathy and connection between the teacher and the student, and I consider this priceless.
And honestly, you’ve got to try teaching others anything in general; it is truly a must-try experience.

How to do it?

Start small.
Choose one skill you know reasonably well.
Find one person who wants to learn it.
Teach in simple steps: explain an idea, show an example, let them try, then give feedback. Don’t over-teach. Focus on progress, not perfection.
And remember, just because you are the teacher, that doesn’t mean you are perfect or know everything; be open-minded, and every idea is worth considering.

How to stay consistent?

Fix a schedule and keep expectations low.
One short session or explanation per week is enough.
Treat mentoring as a habit, not a performance. Show up even when you feel unprepared—teaching itself is what prepares you.

Why are 30 days enough to start it?

Thirty days are enough to teach the basics, repeat key ideas, and notice clear improvement in both the student and the teacher.
It’s enough time to test whether the system works and build a consistent teaching habit.
And also, you will realize how much you’ve gotten better at this skill.

What does success look like after 30 days?

Success is when your student understands things faster, makes fewer basic mistakes, and asks better questions—and when you notice that your own understanding has become clearer and more precise.

What usually goes wrong

People wait until they feel “expert enough,” or they try to teach too much at once. Others quit when students struggle.
Struggle is normal; it’s part of learning, not a sign that teaching isn’t working.

What system or free resources help?

Keep it simple.
Use Google Docs for notes, Discord or Zoom for communication, and Notion for structure if needed.
The system should support teaching, not distract from it. Just keep everything simple.

What transferable lesson does this teach?

Mentoring teaches responsibility, patience, and clarity of thought. It shows that learning deepens when it’s shared—and that sometimes, the greatest growth happens when you help someone else grow.
